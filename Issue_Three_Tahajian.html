Issue Three


A letter to the sugar bowl

Talin Tahajian


You look like a kiss: stained, silent, secular, and yet
the sweep of your stomach seems taut, wary

of sugar. Perhaps it is because God’s cheekbones
are the color of milk—or so they say. Perhaps

you remember my grandmother from Virginia,
a chalky lady, mouth like a sewing needle. She always

claimed to be royalty, thrice removed; we asked her
which side, she claimed that she couldn’t remember.

“Maternal, perhaps.” She would be proud to see you
now, I think, a daughter delaying a pearl of neuroglia

in the attic, another daughter wide awake, coffee
brewing, creamer tipping, and you, sugar bowl, waiting

for daybreak.


Talin Tahajian is the recipient of the Sixth Annual Helen Creeley Poetry Prize, was a finalist in the Nancy Thorp Poetry Contest, and was awarded several regional keys and a national medal in the 2013 Scholastic Art & Writing Awards. She is an executive editor of Polyphony, the creative writing editor of [Slippage] Literary Magazine, and a poetry reader for Postcript. Her work is published in Crashtest and forthcoming from The Winter Tangerine Review, Polyphony, and Postscript. She recently attended the New England Young Writers’ Conference at Bread Loaf, the Kenyon Review Young Writers’ Workshop, and the UVA Young Writers’ Workshop. She will graduate from Belmont High School in 2014.
