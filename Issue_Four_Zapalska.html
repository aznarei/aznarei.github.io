Issue Four


TWO POEMS

Alicja Zapalska



CAPITOL CRAWL

redefined skin slips and scissors its way
down an arm, too heavy to be rootless.

the thaw could not reach this deep.
first the saw of bones and metal;

iron bars pulled out of blooming
windows, curtains resewn. the edges

were sloughed off next: scapula
shaved, tendons rewired.

consider: the turn of the wrist.
a slow, weighted motion captured

as a stuck-out elbow and curved
spine. on the steps, bodies

shed exoskeletons, cumbersome metal
and the weight of their exclusion,

I still have shameful dreams
of this beauty.

PHONE CALL

what makes a person?
An eye, a neck, wide footsteps in the gravel:

Imagine clay shaped in reverse, the heat softening its sides
and swift hands dismantling the body. I think your life

was a little like this. Two rooms, windows crowned with age,
small tea cups wobbling on a rickety green table in

an empty apartment where people keep leaving. The carpets
record the motion, a hole worn bare by the family,

the children, the neighbors who borrow your tools and drop off
bad news. The telephones stopped making sense years ago,

now the kitchen is softer around the edges. You still keep speaking,
speaking to a disconnected number, to the window,

to the family that stops by with champagne and flowers. Outside, the garden
has dried up. You speak into the telephone for water.


Alicja Zapalska moved to America when she was six years old. She finds that the culture shift still haunts her poetry; she began writing as a way of cataloguing two disparate lifestyles, and from there has moved on to discussions of physical impairments and other obsessions that feature in her poems. She is a slam poet and attendee of the Kenyon Review Young Writers Workshop 2013.
