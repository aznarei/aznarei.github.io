Issue Three


TWO POEMS

Amanda Silberling



Evaporation

In mid-November, the city blooms, hot. Pregnant
widows line the streets, virginal, radio static
saying here are record highs, here
are summer clothes, dress light.

Girls become mothers in the still months, infants
shot as cannons. They search for hands in labor,
hands pulling triggers overseas, sweat
dripping down fingers onto magazine
covers with half-mast flags.

In the hospital, birth unfurled you
like a world map, tired and worn. You were
held on display, the men touching over all
the countries they’ve visited.

We’ve never seen the Low Countries.
We drink water from lakes
that are becoming too shallow.

Marjorie

No one was awake when
grandma cooked breakfast,
but we still felt something
tighten around our waists
after she knotted her apron.
She clasps the Star of David
around her neck before
she puts on her glasses.
The torahs on our nightstands
shook. Everything shook in
the morning. Pictures fell
off of the walls: the time
our oldest sister got married
and we brought her flowers,
the time our oldest sister
got divorced and we brought
her flowers. When grandma
turns on the oven, we’re
stirring under our sheets.
It’s morning and there’s
something about a lark going on
but it’s still hard to wake up.


Amanda Silberling lives. Amanda thinks it would be really cool if she just left her blurb at that three-word sentence, but she doesn't have the guts. Amanda has won a National Gold Medal in Poetry from the Scholastic Art and Writing Awards, a commendation for The Poetry Society’s Foyle Young Poets of the Year award, and other prizes. She is the Managing Editor of Winter Tangerine Review, and works on the staffs of The Adroit Journal and Polyphony. Most recently, her work has been published by Houston & Nomadic Voices, Nostrovia!, and Chupa Cabra Press House. Amanda is seventeen years old and resides in South Florida. But most importantly, she lives.
