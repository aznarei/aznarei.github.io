Issue One


TWO POEMS

Stephania Gomez



AN AUTOBIOGRAPHY

I first wisped out like silk curled around corn. They called me
choclo and stuck their fingers through me, wondering for two
years why I stayed short. They didn’t know corn takes twice as
long to grow than to silk.        After that, I learned to stick
my neck up to see a little better and all I saw was more fields.
After five years I was scythed for the first time and it smelled
like chocolate frosting and sharpened Ticonderogas. I grew
long and got dirt on me. I got stuck between pages of chapter
books. Ballerina twists melted in chlorine and dripped off like
new ice.                   One day all I hung towards
were cement sidewalks and dog scat that stuck in their cracks.
I never saw another cornfield. I fainted straight between hot
metal and was severed short again, boiled in red corn syrup,
left alone. Some months I got washed in cool grey lakewater.
No soap. Just smoke suds.      Once I layed waste to dog
shears. Then I dragged in Clorox, then pink, then brown like
Lake Delahey’s mud, then someone’s Pacific Ocean, a color like
husk or Granny Smith, like bruises made in lavender patches,
old Navajo turquoise, the sand on Little Corona beach, Clorox,
Little Corona, blue, blue, Lake Delahey’s mud.        Fuzzy
like moth bodies. Dizzy like earthworms around corn—dippity-
do— they didn’t know corn takes twice as long to worm than
to grow.

9:05 AM

This poem can be found in the in-print version of Issue One.


Stephania Gomez is a sophomore at Brown University who likes wood-canvas canoes and Kathleen Hanna. She is a 2013 YoungArts finalist in poetry and has published in Neurology Today, Renaissance, The Blue Pencil, and NPR's WBEZ/Chicago Amplified, among others. As a spoken word poet, in 2012 she placed 7th out of 800 poets in Louder Than a Bomb, Chicago's teen poetry festival and the largest of its kind in the world, and has performed on the stages of Chicago's Biograph and Steppenwolf Theaters. She has had the opportunity to work closely with such writers as Kevin Coval, Regina Taylor, and David Lee and attended the Iowa Young Writer's Studio in the summer of 2012. She will never write anything as great as this old guy she passed once at a south side funk festival who yelled, mid-pelvic gyration, "OLD SCHOOL FOR LIFE."
